Design Rationale

This visualization explores: How could climate change reshape where Americans choose to vacation by 2100? We built a Vacation Comfort Score from CMIP6 global climate model data, combining temperature and precipitation patterns into a single 0–1 score per US region per year, then projected that score under three different future emissions scenarios.

Why interaction is essential here

A static map freezes time; a static line chart loses geography. The linked choropleth map + time-series chart is the only layout that answers both "where?" and "when?" simultaneously. The season selector reveals patterns invisible in a static view — the South's summer comfort collapses under the worst-case scenario while the Northwest Mountain region's winter score holds up due to continued snowfall. The click-to-detail panel lets users dig into the specific variables driving change in each region. Finally, the guided Climate Story tour walks first-time visitors through the five most important findings automatically, lowering the learning curve without removing any controls for exploratory users.

Visual Encodings

Color uses a binned 6-level RdYlGn scale (not continuous) so readers instantly classify regions as poor/fair/good without reading numbers — binning avoids implying false precision in model output. Position on the line chart encodes comfort score over time for trend detection. Scenario tabs use intuitive emoji + color coding.

Data Transformations

Monthly CMIP6 temperature (tas) and precipitation (pr) variables are aggregated to annual means, then spatially averaged over 4 tourism-oriented US regions using cosine-latitude weighting to correct for grid distortion near the poles. A trapezoidal comfort function with region-specific ideal temperature ranges converts raw values to a 0–1 Vacation Comfort Score.

Alternatives Considered

We considered small-multiples (one map per scenario) but this prevents direct year-by-year comparison. A scatter plot of score vs. year lost geographic context essential for the tourism framing. The click-to-detail panel was added over a persistent sidebar because it keeps the map uncluttered until the user requests depth.

Accessibility & Audience

We deliberately avoided jargon (SSP, TCSI, tas) in favor of plain English ("Best Case", "Vacation Comfort Score", "Worst Case"). Hover tooltips on scenario tabs explain the climate science to general audiences. The click panel contextualizes numbers with plain-language driving factors. The guided Climate Story mode walks new visitors through the five key findings automatically, making the visualization approachable even without prior climate knowledge.

The most time-consuming aspect was preprocessing CMIP6 data — aligning coordinates across scenarios and handling the 0–360° longitude convention. Designing the guided Climate Story tour to feel cinematic without cluttering the interface also required significant iteration, particularly ensuring the year animation, scenario switching, and region highlighting all stayed synchronized. Making the visualization accessible to a general audience through plain-language copy and scenario explanations rounded out the effort.
